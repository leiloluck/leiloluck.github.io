# Cocaine

Cocaine is a powerful CNS stimulant, and most consumed psychoactive plant constituent in the world. Cocaine is known among its users for the 'rush' it provides when first insufflated. It's a crystalline tropane alkaloid that is gathered from the leaves of the coca plant (Erythroxylon coca).

The name comes from "coca" (Quechua "cuca") and "ine" because of its use as a local anesthetic. Later the suffix "-caine" was used to form names of synthetic local anesthetics.

## History

For over a thousand years people have chewed the leaves of Erythroxylon coca, a plant that has many alkaloids, including cocaine.

The isolation of the cocaine alkaloid was not achieved until 1855 by the German chemist Friedrich Gaedcke, who named the alkaloid "Erythroxyline". In 1856 Albert Niemann developed an improved purification process.

In 1879, Vassili von Anrep devised an experiment to demonstrate the analgesic properties of this alkaloid, demonstrating that the substance acted as a local anesthetic.

Later, other substances derived from cocaine, including procaine (Novocaine), lidocaine (Xylocaine), and bupivacaine, were also used as local anesthetics.

## Usage

### Ritual Use
Cocaine is most often associated with the wealthier classes. Consumption of the drug has taken on a strong social character. Cocaine is rarely used by one person alone. When taken with others, the consumption follows a rather well-defined ritual involving lines laid out on a mirror, insufflated through a rolled currency note.

### Crack or Free-Base Cocaine
Crack is smokeable free-base cocaine. It can be obtained from an aqueous solution of cocaine hydrochloride to which an alkaline substance (such as sodium carbonate) is added. A typical dosage ranges from 0.05 to 0.1 g. The effect is very similar to that of snuffed cocaine but is much more intense — lasting only three to five minutes while giving an incredibly strong kick.

### Medicinal Use
Cocaine was initially used for local anesthesia in ophthalmology and dentistry. Because analogs (e.g., procaine) were developed that produce specific effects with no psychoactive side effects, cocaine is rarely used as an anesthetic today.

## Dosage

A "line" of cocaine typically contains between 20 and 100 mg of cocaine, depending on the purity and the consumer's preference. Cocaine promotes compulsive redosing, with many users consuming up to 2-3g within a night — however the comedown will be worse the more cocaine is ingested.

## Effects

Very high dosages of cocaine are said to be able to induce hallucinations. Hallucinations (of nonexistent people, images, flickering lights) often occur during nights in which dosages of 2 to 3 g have been taken. For many people, cocaine also dispels fear. It stimulates a need for alcoholic beverages at the same time that it strongly suppresses the effects of alcohol.

### Addiction
People who use cocaine frequently suffer from a runny nose ("coke sniffles") the following day. Users may counteract this by rinsing their nose with a saline solution. Many users rub vitamin E oil in their nose to regenerate the irritated mucous membranes.

## Chemistry and Pharmacology

Cocaine stimulates the central nervous system, especially the autonomic (sympathetic) system, where it inhibits the reuptake of the neurotransmitters noradrenaline, dopamine, and serotonin and increases the time in which they remain in the synaptic cleft. Cocaine is a triple reuptake inhibitor (SNDRI). It also acts as local anesthetic. It has strong stimulant and vasoconstricting properties.

### Production
Under optimal conditions, it is possible to produce 1 kg of pure cocaine from 100 kg of coca leaves. The methods of extracting cocaine from the coca plant utilize an acid/base extraction method.

## Legal Status

Possession of cocaine without a medical prescription is illegal pretty much worldwide.
