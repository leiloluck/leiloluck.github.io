# Ketamine

**Source:** https://wiki.tripsit.me/wiki/Ketamine
**Downloaded:** 2026-02-20
**Language:** English

---

Ketamine is a dissociative anesthetic that belongs to the arylcyclohexylamine class, and commonly used in human medical and veterinary care. It has a very wide safety margin, with an anesthetic dose being as much as ten times a recreational dose in an inexperienced user. Ketamine is generally sold to the recreational user in one of two forms: in the evaporated salts, which are sold as a powder, or crystals (often referred to as shards), as well as in multi-dose vials for use in veterinary and human medicine.

One manufacturer, Parke-Davis, reports cases of accidental overdoses of ketamine as much as ten times higher than that required for surgery (which is to say 40-100x a recreational dose) "with no obvious, lasting effects." It is, therefore, reasonable to consider it a drug of relatively safe usage. Because ketamine is a dissociative and an anesthetic, users are prone to becoming injured by interaction with their environment and have died from things such as drowning. Overdose, however, is unlikely.

## History

Ketamine was originally produced by Parke-Davis laboratories in the early 1960s, and its recreational use was first reported in 1965; by the early 1970s, the US FDA was concerned about its use as a recreational drug. In the early 1990s, the US "Drug Czar" labeled ketamine as an "emerging drug" because of its involvement in the electronic music scene, and by 1999, it had been scheduled in the United States (Schedule III), although this designation is only applicable when the drug is intended for use in humans.

## Dosage

Ketamine is usually either injected intramuscularly or insufflated. Additional routes of administration are "plugging" (rectal) and orally. Oral availability of ketamine is poor.

### A Note on the "K-hole"

There is no "guaranteed" dose to "hole" with ketamine. The "k-hole" is a state of full dissociative anesthesia in which the user is able to retain a semblance of consciousness. The effect associated with this is ego death; the dissolution of the ego, the loss of the perspective of "I" in perception. Reaching too far with dosage will result in full anesthesia without memory of the experience. Too low a dosage will result in a mild sedation and body load, but no ego death.

It is posited that using a needle and a precisely measured dose is more likely to get a user to a full state of ego death due to the lack of titration of dosage, rapid come-up, and exactly-metered dosage.

### Oral
1-4mg/lb of body mass. Doses higher than 3mg/lb may exceed the recreational window. Doses of 2-3mg/lb may incur greater short-term memory loss.

### Insufflated
Threshold effects may begin as low as 5mg, and recreational doses can range up to 150mg, with common doses being 30-75mg. Insufflated doses above 100mg are often enough to enter a K-Hole. Ketamine is reported to be fairly gentle on the nasal tissues.

### Intramuscular Injection
Typically 1mg/lb body mass for IM injection is quite sufficient for a full dissociative experience. Doses of .5-.75mg/lb are more "threshold" experiences. Doses exceeding 1mg/lb body mass usually result in full anesthesia with little recall.

### Intravenous Injection
Intravenous injection is possible with ketamine. The doses are the same as for intramuscular injection. However, the user can become fully anesthetized before being able to remove the needle. For this reason alone intravenous injection of ketamine is discouraged.

## Effects

### Positive
- Ego death — ketamine is often considered to be a very quick, repeatable, safe route to ego death.
- Some users report euphoria.
- Pain relief.
- There has been some indication that ketamine, in low doses (20-30mg), can be an effective anti-depressant.

### Negative
- Long-term, chronic usage may lead to psychosis.
- Bladder and lower urinary tract discomfort, up to and including tissue necrosis requiring replacement of bladder, ureters, and urethra. If these effects are noticed, discontinue use immediately.
- Ketamine is well-known in the psychedelics community to be habit-forming. Psychological dependence is an issue that is widely reported.
- Any substance used intramuscularly or intravenously can be associated with abscesses among other hazards.

### After Effects
Ketamine takes much longer to return to a complete baseline than is immediately apparent to the user. While major effects subside in usually less than an hour, some research has shown it can take a day or longer for more subtle psychological effects to subside.

## Chemistry and Pharmacology

### Pharmacokinetics
Ketamine is absorbable via intravenous, intramuscular, oral, and topical routes due to both its water and lipid solubilities. When administered orally, it undergoes first-pass metabolism in the liver by CYP3A4 (major), CYP2B6 (minor), and CYP2C9 (minor) isoenzymes into norketamine and finally dehydronorketamine.

Bioavailability: 93% intramuscularly, 25–50% intranasally, 30% sublingually, 30% rectally, 17-20% orally.

Peak plasma concentrations: within a minute intravenously, 5–15 min intramuscularly, 30 min orally.

Duration: 30 min to 2 h intramuscularly, 4–6 h orally.

### Pharmacodynamics
Pharmacologically, ketamine is classified as an NMDA receptor antagonist. At high, fully anesthetic level doses, ketamine has also been found to bind to μ-opioid receptors type 2. Ketamine also interacts with muscarinic receptors, descending monoaminergic pain pathways and voltage-gated calcium channels.

## Legal Status

- United States: Schedule III
- In some countries, such as Thailand and Mexico, ketamine is available over-the-counter without a prescription.
